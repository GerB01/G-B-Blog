Chapter I · Foundations

What is CRO?

Conversion Rate Optimization is not a marketing tactic — it is the discipline of removing the gap between intent and outcome.

Note on examples: The before/after case studies throughout this book are illustrative composites based on common, documented CRO patterns. They are presented with realistic specifics to make the learnings concrete — not as verbatim client case studies.

The working definition

Conversion Rate Optimization (CRO) is the structured practice of increasing the percentage of visitors who complete a desired action — buying, signing up, booking, downloading, replying. CRO is research-led, hypothesis-driven, and measured. It is not “redesigning the homepage.” It is not “making the button green.” It is the slow, accountable work of turning more of the traffic you already have into business outcomes.

The formula

The math is uncomplicated, which is part of why it gets misused:

Conversion Rate = (Conversions ÷ Total Visitors) × 100

If 1,000 visitors arrive and 23 buy, your conversion rate is 2.3%. The trap is that one number hides everything that matters: which traffic source converts, which device, which page, which step in the funnel. A site-wide CR of 2.3% can contain a 9% rate on returning desktop traffic and a 0.4% rate on paid mobile — and the actions you take are entirely different for each. Always segment before you celebrate or panic.

Why traffic alone is not enough

Doubling traffic doubles cost. Doubling conversion rate doubles revenue at the same cost. If you spend $5,000/month on ads and convert at 1.5%, lifting that rate to 3% is the equivalent of acquiring $5,000 of free traffic — every month, compounding. CRO is the only growth lever that improves the ROI of every other growth lever you already have: SEO, paid, partnerships, email. That is why it is the highest-leverage work most teams systematically underinvest in.

Before / After
SaaS pricing page — segment-aware optimization

Context · B2B SaaS, $49–$299/mo plans

BeforeSingle pricing page treated all traffic the same. Site-wide CR reported as 1.8%, masking a 4.1% rate on direct traffic and 0.6% on paid social.

Chapter II · Framework

The 4 Pillars

Every durable CRO program rests on the same four pillars. Skip one and the work becomes guessing dressed up as strategy.

i.

Conversion Research

Quantitative + qualitative diagnosis of where users drop off, get confused, or hesitate. The “what” and the “why” before any change.

ii.

Persuasion

Applying behavioural science — clarity, framing, social proof, urgency — to messaging, offer structure, and visual hierarchy.

iii.

A/B Testing

Validating change through controlled experiments to separate signal from opinion, intuition, and HiPPO bias.

iv.

UX & Friction Reduction

Stripping every avoidable cost from the user’s path: extra clicks, redundant fields, slow loads, unclear copy.

How they work together

Research generates hypotheses. Persuasion shapes the proposed solution. A/B testing validates the change. UX governs the execution. Skip research and you test random ideas. Skip persuasion and you ship technically clean pages no one acts on. Skip testing and you are renovating in the dark. Skip UX and your beautifully-worded page still converts at 0.3% because the form takes 14 seconds to load on 4G.

Before / After
Lead-gen agency site — research-led rewrite

Context · B2B services, ~8,000 monthly visits

BeforeTeam rebuilt the homepage based on internal opinion. Polished visuals, no measurable lift over 90 days. CR stuck at 1.1%.
AfterRan 12 customer interviews + Hotjar session reviews. Discovered users couldn’t tell what the agency actually did. Rewrote H1 from “Strategy that scales” to “We rebuild your funnel and guarantee a 30% lift in 90 days — or refund.”

Result: form submissions rose +74%; qualified-lead rate per submission also improved.

Chapter III · Behavioural Science

The Psychology of Conversion

People don’t decide on what’s true; they decide on what’s easy to evaluate. Persuasion is the work of making the right choice the easy one.

Cialdini’s six principles, correctly applied

Robert Cialdini’s six principles of influence — documented in Influence (1984) — map cleanly to conversion levers. The principles are: Reciprocity, Commitment & Consistency, Social Proof, Authority, Liking, and Scarcity. CRO practitioners commonly add Urgency as an applied form of scarcity (time-bound rather than quantity-bound), but it is not one of Cialdini’s original six. The trick is execution, not awareness.

  • Social Proof — humans look to others to decide what’s safe. Replace generic “trusted by thousands” with named, photographed testimonials, embedded G2 or Trustpilot widgets, real customer logos (not stock badges), and counters like “1,247 booked this week” via Proof or Fomo.
  • Scarcity & Urgency — rare and time-limited = more valuable. Display “3 seats left” only when true. Show “Last booked 6 minutes ago” for hotel-style bookings. Use real deadlines: cohort close dates, limited-run pricing, expiring bonuses. Never fake countdown timers — users recognise them and trust collapses. Urgency is time-scarcity; scarcity is quantity-scarcity. Both work; both must be real.
  • Reciprocity — giving creates obligation. Lead with a free audit, calculator, template, or first chapter — not a “request a demo” gate. Tools like Tally and Notion templates are easy reciprocity-builders.
  • Authority — credentials shortcut trust. Show press logos, certifications, podcast appearances, and named experts. A founder photo near the CTA outperforms anonymous brand voice.
  • Commitment & Consistency — small yeses lead to big yeses. Multi-step forms (Tally, Typeform) leverage this by getting the easy agreement first, the commitment last.
  • Liking — people say yes to people (and brands) they like. Use a relatable founder story, a real team page, and a brand voice that sounds like a person, not a committee.

The 3–8 second window

Empirical dwell-time research (Nielsen Norman Group, Chartbeat) consistently shows the bounce decision happens within 3–8 seconds depending on traffic source and page intent. The “8-second rule” is a widely-used CRO heuristic — useful as a design constraint even if the exact figure varies. To pass that test, your hero must answer three questions in plain copy: What is this? Who is it for? What do I do next? If a visitor can’t answer all three with the page muted and scrolled to the top, you have lost them. Read your H1 aloud and ask: would a 12-year-old understand it in one read?

Trust signals that actually work

Trust is built in the small things visitors notice unconsciously: a real founder photo (not stock), an HTTPS padlock, a real address in the footer, a money-back guarantee in plain English, named author bylines on content, named clients (not “Fortune 500 brands”), and recent dates (“Updated April 2026”). One missing element doesn’t kill trust; the cumulative absence of all of them does.

AfterReplaced badges with embedded Trustpilot widget (4.7★, 2,341 reviews), added founder video below add-to-cart, and a “Loved by 14,000+ customers since 2022” counter.

Chapter IV · Diagnostics

Research & Discovery

You cannot optimise a page you do not understand. Every winning test starts with a specific, observed problem — never with a hunch.

Heatmaps

Use Hotjar or Microsoft Clarity (free) to capture click, scroll, and movement maps on your top 3 pages: homepage, primary product/landing page, and checkout/contact page. Look specifically for: clicks on non-clickable elements (a sign of unclear hierarchy), low scroll depth on long pages (your most important content may be below the fold), and “rage clicks” (Clarity flags these automatically — they indicate broken or confusing UI).

Session recordings

Watch at least 10 non-converter recordings before drawing any conclusions — more if traffic volume allows. Filter for sessions that did not convert and lasted longer than 30 seconds. At low-traffic sites, every recording counts; at high-traffic sites, use rage-click and dead-click filters to surface the most informative sessions. Note timestamps where users hesitate, scroll back up, or hover indecisively. Two patterns repeated across 3+ recordings = a hypothesis.

User surveys

Run two surveys, always. First, an on-exit survey via Hotjar or Sleeknote: “What stopped you from [action] today?” Second, a post-purchase survey via Typeform: “What almost stopped you from buying?” These two questions, asked of 100+ respondents, will yield friction points more accurate than any analytics dashboard. Even 20–30 consistent qualitative responses can be enough to identify actionable patterns.

Funnel analysis

In GA4, build a Funnel Exploration with each step of your primary journey — landing → product/pricing → form → submission. The biggest drop-off step is your highest-leverage problem. If you lose 80% between pricing and form-load, the answer is on the pricing page (objection unanswered) or the form (too long, broken on mobile).

Heuristic reviews

Walk every primary page yourself with a structured checklist: clarity (can I describe what this page wants in one sentence?), value proposition, friction, distraction, anxiety triggers, and trust signals. CXL’s LIFT model is a solid starting framework. Do this before opening analytics — fresh eyes catch what numbers can’t.

Before / After
SaaS onboarding — session recording insight

Context · Self-serve free trial signup

BeforeTrial-to-paid conversion sat at 4.2%. Team assumed product-fit issue.
AfterWatched 30 Clarity recordings. 11/30 users tried to click the “feature preview” image expecting it to be interactive. Replaced it with a 30-second Loom walkthrough and added a “Start a sample project” CTA.

Result: trial activation rose +38%; trial-to-paid moved from 4.2% to 6.1% within one cycle.

Chapter V · Reading the Signals

How to Read Your Data

A dashboard full of numbers is not insight. The skill is knowing which three metrics actually move the business — and what to do when each one moves.

The metrics that actually matter

In GA4, focus on a tight set: conversion rate (per source, per device), engagement rate, scroll depth, exit pages, average engagement time, and funnel step completion. In Hotjar/Clarity, focus on: rage clicks, dead clicks, scroll depth heatmap, and recording filters for “didn’t convert + spent > 30s.” Anything else is a vanity metric for now.

Bounce rate vs. engagement rate

GA4 replaced the old bounce rate with engagement rate, and most teams still misread it. Engagement rate = sessions that lasted 10+ seconds, fired a conversion event, OR had 2+ pageviews. Bounce rate in GA4 is simply the inverse (1 − engagement rate). A 65% engagement rate on a long-form blog is healthy; a 65% engagement rate on a checkout page is a fire. The action depends on intent: high bounce on top-of-funnel means weak hook; on bottom-of-funnel it means broken trust or pricing shock.

Scroll depth

Track 25/50/75/90% scroll milestones in GA4 (enable Enhanced Measurement, then add custom event triggers in GTM if needed). If 90% of users never reach 50% scroll on a long landing page, your H1 and hero are failing the first-impression test — they aren’t earning the scroll. The fix is the hero, not the rest of the page.

Exit pages

The Pages and Screens report in GA4 shows where users leave. An exit on /thank-you is fine. An exit on /checkout/payment is critical. Cross-reference exit pages with Hotjar recordings of those specific URLs to see why. Common culprits: surprise shipping costs, broken payment fields on mobile, or required-account walls.

Funnel drop-off

Build the funnel exploration in GA4 (Explore → Funnel exploration). The single biggest percentage drop is where to spend the next 30 days of CRO effort. If 100 land → 60 view product → 18 add to cart → 3 buy, the cart-to-buy step (83% drop) is leaking money. Hypotheses to test there: shipping cost surprise, guest-checkout missing, payment options too narrow, trust signals absent at the moment of payment.

What each metric tells you — and what to do

  • Low engagement rate (<50%) on landing pages → message-match problem between ad and landing. Action: rewrite the H1 to mirror the ad copy verbatim.
  • Shallow scroll depth (<25%) on long pages → hero failing the first-impression test. Action: rewrite hero copy and add a visible “scroll” cue.
  • High exit on a mid-funnel page → unanswered objection. Action: add an FAQ block addressing the top 3 objections from your post-purchase survey.
  • High cart abandonment (70%+ is typical; 85%+ is broken) → friction or sticker shock. Baymard Institute’s 2024 aggregate puts the average at ~70% — so if you’re at 70% you’re normal, not fine. Action: show full cost (incl. shipping) earlier, add Apple/Google Pay, exit-intent offer.
  • Mobile CR <50% of desktop CR → mobile UX broken. Action: real-device test on iPhone SE; check tap targets, font sizes, sticky CTA.

BeforeOwner stared at 1.2% site-wide CR for months. Ran 4 unrelated tests, all flat.
AfterBuilt GA4 funnel: 100 → landing 78 → curriculum 22 → checkout 3. Massive drop at curriculum step. Watched Clarity recordings — users got stuck reading 14 modules. Replaced curriculum table with a 60-second Loom walkthrough + outcome bullets.

Chapter VI · Experimentation

A/B Testing

A/B testing isn’t a button you press. It’s a discipline of isolating one variable, running it long enough, and being honest about what the math says.

How to run a test correctly

Every legitimate test has six elements: a written hypothesis (“If we change X to Y, then Z metric will improve because [behavioural reason]”), a single variable changed, a defined primary metric, a calculated minimum sample size, a fixed test duration of at least one full business cycle (typically 2 weeks minimum), and a pre-committed decision rule. Tools: VWO, Convert.com, Statsig, or AB Tasty. (Google Optimize was sunset in September 2023 — do not use it.)

Isolating variables

Change one thing at a time. If you swap the headline AND the CTA color AND the hero image and the new variant wins, you have learned nothing about why. Sequence variable changes: test headline first, declare a winner, then test CTA against the new winner, then image. This is slower and the only way to build a body of validated learnings instead of a graveyard of “we tried that once.”

Statistical significance

Aim for 95% confidence (p ≤ 0.05) and a calculated sample size that detects your minimum meaningful effect. Use Optimizely’s or VWO’s free sample size calculators. A 5% lift on a page that gets 200 conversions/month requires several months of data to reach significance — accept that and don’t peek early. Stopping a test the moment significance flickers green (“peeking”) inflates false positives dramatically.

When NOT to test

Don’t A/B test when: traffic is below roughly 500 weekly conversions on the test page (you’ll almost never reach significance in a reasonable window — do qualitative research instead); the change is obviously broken (a 404 doesn’t need a test); you’re testing at the same time as a major paid campaign launch or seasonal event (confound); or the change is mandated by legal, brand, or accessibility requirements — just ship it. Below 500 weekly conversions, prioritise bigger hypothesis-driven swings backed by qualitative evidence over attempting statistical rigour.

Before / After
E-commerce CTA copy test — single-variable discipline

Context · DTC apparel, high-traffic PDP

BeforeTeam wanted to “refresh” the product page — new image, new copy, new button color in one sweep. Test “won” but couldn’t be diagnosed or replicated.
AfterReset and tested only CTA copy: “Add to bag” vs. “Add to bag — free returns.” Ran for 21 days on 38,000 visitors. Clean win at 96% confidence.

Result: add-to-cart rate +11.4%; learning logged and applied site-wide.

Chapter VII · Pages That Convert

Landing Page Optimization

A landing page has one job: walk one specific visitor from one specific intent to one specific action. Everything else is noise.

Headlines

Your H1 must answer “what is this and what do I get?” in under 12 words. Use the formula: [Outcome the user wants] + [time/effort qualifier] + [credibility hook]. Example: “Get a fully-coded landing page in 7 days — built and conversion-tested by senior devs.” Avoid clever-but-empty wordplay; you have roughly 3–8 seconds to be understood, not admired. Test outcome-led (“Double your form submissions”) vs. mechanism-led (“Multi-step Tally forms that finish”) to find which framing your audience responds to.

CTAs

One primary CTA per page, repeated 3–5 times down the scroll. Use first-person, outcome-focused copy: “Get my free audit,” “Show me the template,” “Start my 14-day trial.” Avoid “Submit,” “Click here,” “Learn more.” Button color matters less than contrast — your CTA should be the highest-contrast element on the page. Add microcopy reassurance directly below (“No credit card required,” “30-day refund — no questions”).

Form length

Every field is a decision the user must make. Published HubSpot form research is consistent: dropping from 11 fields to 4 typically lifts form completion 30–50%. Audit ruthlessly: do you actually use “company size” in your follow-up? If not, cut it. Use progressive profiling — capture email first, ask the rest after they’re committed. For multi-step flows, use Tally or Typeform; conversion lifts of 20–40% over single-page forms are common.

Above the fold

Above the fold (roughly the first 600 px on mobile, 800 px on desktop) must contain: clear H1, supporting subhead, primary CTA, and one trust signal (a press logo strip, a star rating, or a named testimonial). Resist the urge to put your entire pitch up top — the goal is to earn the scroll, not deliver the whole story instantly.

Mobile-first

Design and test mobile first; adapt to desktop after. Most B2C and a growing share of B2B traffic is mobile. Specifics: tap targets ≥ 48×48 px (Google’s threshold), body text ≥ 16 px to prevent iOS auto-zoom, sticky CTA bar after the user scrolls past the hero, single-column layout, no hover-only interactions, and fonts that load with font-display: swap.

Before / After
Lead-gen landing page — form length cut

Context · B2B SaaS demo request

BeforeDemo form had 11 fields including phone, company size, role, use case, and timeline. Form-load to submit rate: 4.1%.
AfterCut to 3 fields (work email, name, company URL). Auto-enriched the rest via HubSpot Breeze Intelligence (formerly Clearbit) or Apollo.io on the backend. Sales team got the same data; users gave it without seeing it.

Result: form completion rose to 11.6%. Qualified-demo rate per submission dropped slightly but absolute qualified demos +2.3×.

Chapter VIII · Anti-Patterns

Common Mistakes Developers Make

Most conversion problems aren’t strategy failures — they’re a series of small, avoidable engineering and design defaults shipped without a second thought.

Optimising for desktop when most traffic is mobile

Builders work on desktop monitors, so they design for desktop. Then they ship to an audience that’s 65–75% mobile. Open Chrome DevTools, set the viewport to iPhone SE (375×667), and rebuild from there. If something feels cramped on iPhone SE, it is cramped — that device is still in active use globally and represents the floor.

Changing too many things at once

“We redesigned the homepage and CR went up 15%.” Cool — what caused it? Was it the H1, the new image, the layout, the typography, or the page-speed improvements that came with the rebuild? You’ll never know, and you’ll never replicate it. Sequence changes; isolate variables; log learnings.

Trusting gut over data

The HiPPO (Highest Paid Person’s Opinion) is the most expensive mistake in CRO. Replace “I think users will…” with “We saw 9 of 14 session recordings where users…” Always bring data to the meeting. If you don’t have data, run a 5-day Hotjar survey before opining.

Confusing pretty with effective

Awwwards-style hero animations look beautiful in a portfolio and crater on conversion. Awards reward novelty; users reward clarity. If your hero takes 2 seconds to “reveal,” your bounce rate just rose. Static, fast, clear > animated, slow, clever — every time.

Ignoring page speed

Each additional second of load time can drop conversions 7–10% (research from Akamai, Portent, and Google consistently shows this direction; exact figures vary by site and audience). Run Google PageSpeed Insights monthly. Compress images with Squoosh or ShortPixel, lazy-load below-the-fold media via loading="lazy", defer non-critical JavaScript, subset Google Fonts to only used weights, and serve from a CDN (Cloudflare’s free tier is enough for most sites).

Not testing forms end-to-end

Submit your own form on a real iPhone, on cellular (not Wi-Fi), with autofill enabled. Watch what breaks: keyboard hides the submit button, validation errors appear off-screen, the success message flashes for 200ms then redirects. Most of these are invisible on desktop QA. Schedule an end-to-end form test once a month and after any deploy that touches the form.

Before / After

Chapter X · The Business of CRO

CRO for Freelancers & Clients

Selling CRO is selling certainty. Clients don’t pay you to redesign — they pay you to take the risk out of decisions they were already going to make.

How to pitch CRO audits

Don’t sell “an audit.” Sell a forecast of recovered revenue. Pre-audit, ask three questions: monthly traffic, current conversion rate, and average order value. Multiply: a site with 50,000 visits/month at 1.5% CR and $80 AOV is doing $60k/mo. A 30% relative CR lift = $18k/mo recovered. Charge 1–3 months of recovered revenue as a flat fee. Lead with the math, not the methodology.

What to deliver

A professional CRO audit deliverable contains: an executive summary (one page, plain English, with the revenue forecast); a heuristic walkthrough of the top 3 pages with annotated screenshots; quantitative findings from GA4 (funnel drops, source-segmented CR); qualitative findings from session recordings + an exit survey; a prioritised list of 8–15 hypotheses ranked by ICE score (Impact × Confidence × Ease); and a 90-day roadmap with sequencing. Use the CRO Audit Template below as the spine.

How to frame results

Frame everything in revenue, not percentages. “We lifted CR from 1.8% to 2.4%” is forgettable. “We recovered $14,200/month in lost revenue without spending an extra dollar on traffic” is what gets quoted to the CEO. Build a one-page monthly report: starting CR, ending CR, dollars recovered (run-rate), tests run, tests won. Keep it short — a one-pager renews; a 40-slide deck doesn’t.

Before / After
Freelance positioning shift — outcome over deliverable

Context · Independent consultant, B2B clients

BeforePitch deck led with “60-page CRO audit + 12 prioritised recommendations.” Closed roughly 1 in 8 calls. Average project value $2,500.
AfterPitch deck led with “We forecast and recover at least $X/month in lost revenue. If we can’t, you don’t pay.” Same audit; different framing.

Result: close rate climbed to 1 in 3; average project value to $11,400.

Chapter XI · Equipment

Tools & Stack

Tools don’t make conversions; operators do. But the right tools shorten the loop between question and answer — pick a small stack and master it.

Hotjar

Best for combined heatmaps + recordings + surveys in one interface. Set up: install snippet via GTM, enable click + scroll heatmaps on top 3 pages, run a 5-day on-exit survey: “What stopped you from [action] today?” Watch recordings filtered to rage clicks + non-converters. Check Hotjar’s current pricing tiers on their site — plans change periodically.

Microsoft Clarity

Free, unlimited recordings and heatmaps with automatic rage-click and dead-click flags. Lighter than Hotjar but no surveys. Best as a free baseline or as a complement (run Hotjar for surveys, Clarity for unlimited recording capture). Install via GTM in 5 minutes.

Google Optimize — discontinued

Google sunset Optimize in September 2023. If you see tutorials referencing it, ignore them. Replacements: VWO (full-featured, paid), Convert.com (privacy-friendly, fair pricing for mid-market), AB Tasty (enterprise), Statsig (engineering-led teams), and GrowthBook (open-source, self-hosted).

VWO

Use when you need a polished WYSIWYG editor for non-developers and want heatmaps, recordings, funnels, and surveys under one roof. Viable as the only tool in the stack for many mid-market teams. Check their site for current traffic-tier pricing.

GA4

Mandatory baseline. Set up: install via GTM, enable Enhanced Measurement (scroll, outbound clicks, file downloads), mark CTA clicks as conversion events, build at least one Funnel Exploration for your primary journey, and configure UTM tagging on every paid link. Use DebugView during setup to verify events fire correctly. If you only set up GA4 once, do it during install — retrofitting after 6 months loses 6 months of data.

When to use what

  • Starting fresh, low budget → GA4 + Microsoft Clarity + Tally for forms.
  • Mid-market, >50k visits/mo → GA4 + Hotjar + VWO or Convert.com.
  • Engineering-led, custom stack → GA4 + Clarity + Statsig or GrowthBook (self-hosted).
  • Enterprise / regulated → GA4 + Contentsquare + AB Tasty + Qualtrics for surveys.

⌘ Glossary

Glossary

One-line definitions for every key term used in this book — for quick reference, not theory.

Conversion Rate
The percentage of visitors who complete a desired action: (conversions ÷ visitors) × 100.
A/B Testing
A controlled experiment comparing two versions of a page or element to measure which performs better against a defined metric at a specified confidence level.
Statistical Significance
The confidence threshold (typically 95%) at which a measured difference between variants is unlikely to be due to random chance (p ≤ 0.05).
Heuristic Analysis
A structured expert review of a page using a framework (e.g. CXL’s LIFT model) to identify clarity, friction, and trust gaps without analytics.
Micro-Conversion
A smaller user action that signals progress toward the macro goal — newsletter signup, video play, scroll to 75%.
Macro-Conversion
The primary business goal of the page — purchase, demo request, paid trial, booking.
Heatmap
A visual aggregation of user clicks, scrolls, or movement on a page, used to spot attention patterns and dead zones.
Session Recording
A replayable capture of one anonymous visitor’s mouse movement, clicks, and scrolling through a site.
Bounce Rate (GA4)
The inverse of engagement rate — sessions that did not engage (under 10s, no conversion event, single page). Not the same as the Universal Analytics definition.
Engagement Rate (GA4)
Sessions lasting 10+ seconds, OR firing a conversion event, OR with 2+ pageviews — divided by total sessions.
Funnel
The ordered set of steps a user takes from arrival to conversion; analysed to find and fix the largest drop-off step.
CTA
Call to action — the button, link, or instruction prompting the user toward the next step.
Above the Fold
The visible area of a page on first load before scrolling — roughly the first 600 px on mobile, 800 px on desktop.
Social Proof
Evidence that other people use, trust, or endorse the product — reviews, logos, testimonials, user counters.
Friction
Any avoidable cost (cognitive, physical, emotional) the user must pay to complete the action — extra fields, slow loads, unclear copy.
Landing Page
A standalone page built for one specific traffic source and one specific intent, with a single primary action.
ICE Score
A prioritisation framework: Impact × Confidence × Ease, used to rank hypotheses in a CRO test backlog.
Hypothesis
A written prediction in the form: “If we change X to Y, then Z metric will improve because [behavioural reason].”
Message Match
The degree to which the language and offer on a landing page mirror the ad or referrer that brought the user there.
HiPPO
Highest Paid Person’s Opinion — the tendency of teams to defer to seniority rather than data when making design decisions.
Rage Click
A user clicking the same element multiple times in rapid succession, typically flagged by Clarity or Hotjar as a sign of confusion or broken UI.
